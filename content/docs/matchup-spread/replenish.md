## General Plan

**Replenish** is probably one of the most iconic examples of a near fifty-fifty combo matchup.

Both decks can be very fast. hFEB can virtually have a turn two or three kill with **Hermit Druid**, while Replenish can quickly assemble its graveyard and resolve a single spell that completely flips the game.

The main difficulty of the matchup is that many of Replenish's combo cards naturally interact with your plan. These aren't simple sideboard cards or dedicated answers: they're pieces of their deck that, while advancing their game plan, also disrupt yours.

For example:

- **Opalescence** turns **Survival of the Fittest** into a creature, exposing it to **Swords to Plowshares**;
- **Seal of Cleansing** can destroy Survival;
- **Parallax Wave** can exile Hermit Druid or other creature-based pieces;
- **Swords to Plowshares** cleanly interacts with Hermit Druid and Volrath's Shapeshifter;
- **Meddling Mage**, main or side depending on the list, can shut off a key piece;
- **Abeyance** can be lethal against Hermit lines.

This is what makes the matchup complicated: Replenish doesn't necessarily have to deviate from its own plan to interact with you. Many of its best cards are already good against hFEB.

## Game 1

### A True Race Matchup

Game 1 is often a race.

If the opponent doesn't have an answer to **Hermit Druid**, you can win very quickly. In many games, Druid is your best way to stay ahead of the Replenish plan, because it forces the opponent to immediately have **Swords to Plowshares**, **Parallax Wave**, or some form of relevant interaction.

The problem is that Replenish, even in Game 1, has many cards that can answer both of your main plans.

Against **Hermit Druid**, they can have:

- Swords to Plowshares;
- Parallax Wave;
- Abeyance, if present;
- Meddling Mage, in some versions.

Against **Survival of the Fittest**, they can have:

- Seal of Cleansing;
- Opalescence + Swords to Plowshares;
- Meddling Mage;
- counters in the lists that run them.

This means that, even in Game 1, the opponent can interact with both the Hermit plan and the Survival plan. That's where the fifty-fifty feel comes from: a significant portion of games is decided by how well the opponent's answers line up against your initial plan.

### Why Replenish Interacts So Well

The most important thing to understand is that Replenish's combo pieces are also interaction pieces.

**Seal of Cleansing** is a card Replenish wants to play anyway, but against you it also becomes a direct answer to Survival.

**Parallax Wave** is part of their Opalescence plan, but it can also shut off Hermit Druid or temporarily remove key creatures.

**Opalescence** is the card that turns their board into a lethal threat, but against Survival it also has a very dangerous indirect function: by turning it into a creature, it makes Survival removable with Swords to Plowshares.

This means it isn't enough to ask *"does the opponent have hate?"*. Very often the hate is already inside their natural plan.

## Cabal Therapy and Discard

Discard spells are fundamental in the matchup.

With **Cabal Therapy**, the most common Game 1 call is often **Swords to Plowshares**. The reason is that Swords always interacts with Hermit Druid and, in some board configurations, can also interact with Survival after Opalescence.

On top of that, many lists run more copies of Swords than Seal of Cleansing. For example, some versions have four Swords and two Seal, so statistically Swords is often the most likely and most transversal card.

That said, you shouldn't always name Swords automatically.

If your hand is slower and the plan goes through **Survival of the Fittest**, it can be correct to name **Seal of Cleansing**. This is especially true if you need more turns to use Survival and assemble a line. In that case, Seal is the card that can make you lose time and resources before you manage to convert Survival into a win.

The rule is:

> If the plan is Hermit Druid, you often name Swords.
> If the plan is Survival and you need more turns, Seal of Cleansing can be the better call.

As always, Therapy has to name the card that beats your specific line, not just the strongest card in the abstract.

## Abeyance

**Abeyance** is one of the most dangerous cards to respect.

The main problem arises in **Hermit Druid** lines. If you activate Druid in upkeep and the opponent responds with Abeyance, you can no longer cast **Krosan Reclamation** to put cards back into the library. At that point, you mill the entire library and lose on the draw step.

The sequence is very clear:

> You activate Hermit Druid in upkeep.
> The opponent casts Abeyance in response.
> You can no longer cast Krosan Reclamation.
> The library ends up in the graveyard.
> You go to the draw step and lose.

For this reason you shouldn't die to Abeyance unless forced. If the opponent isn't applying immediate pressure and you aren't required to force a risky Hermit line, it can be correct to wait, use discard, or look for a different plan.

The presence of Abeyance varies a lot by list. Some versions run three main deck, others two in the sideboard, others none. But it's a card to always keep in mind when the opponent represents white mana.

## Counterspell and Mana Leak / Miscalculation Versions

Some versions of Replenish run light counters, like:

- **Mana Leak**;
- **Miscalculation**.

They aren't always present, and in general they aren't the center of the matchup. For this reason, you usually shouldn't consider **Xantid Swarm** a fundamental sideboard card in this matchup. It's often not relevant enough, because the main interactions aren't counters but white permanents, removal, Abeyance, and combo pieces.

If you know the opponent has many counters, the evaluation can shift slightly, but in general you should prefer increasing discard, enchantment answers, and removal for Meddling Mage rather than leaning on Xantid.

## Meddling Mage

**Meddling Mage** is one of the main post-board problems, and in some lists it can already be present main deck.

Mage is annoying because it does two things at once:

- it shuts off a fundamental piece of your plan;
- it puts a clock on the board.

The clock seems unimportant, but it isn't. A 2/2 attacking every turn forces you not to lose too much time, especially because your manabase can deal you damage and because you still have to find cards to prevent Replenish from assembling its plan.

If you don't remove Mage quickly, you can find yourself in the situation where you simultaneously have to:

- resolve the name Mage chose;
- protect Survival or Hermit;
- prevent Replenish;
- not die to the clock.

For this reason, post-board you want dedicated removal.

The best cards are:

- **Swords to Plowshares**;
- **Ghitu Slinger**;
- possibly **Pyroclasm**, though personally not ideal.

Pyroclasm can answer Meddling Mage, but it isn't a great fit because it can be situational and doesn't always line up well with the rest of the plan. Personally, more targeted or tutorable removal is preferable.

## Post-Sideboard

Post-board the matchup becomes more complicated for both players.

On your side, you want to bring in:

- additional discard, like **Duress**;
- enchantment removal;
- **Monk Realist**;
- **Naturalize**;
- **Ray of Revelation**;
- removal for Meddling Mage, like Swords or Ghitu Slinger;
- possibly flexible answers that can also hit **Tormod's Crypt**.

**Naturalize** is particularly useful because it can answer both problematic enchantments and possible artifacts like Crypt.

**Ray of Revelation** is strong because it can break two enchantments over the course of the game, but you have to remember that Replenish is a deck that can interact a lot with the graveyard and with the timing of your resources. The flashback isn't always automatically free.

**Monk Realist** is very interesting because it's a tutorable answer with Survival and can remove key enchantments.

### Gloom

**Gloom** is one of the best sideboard cards against Replenish.

The common thread of many of the opponent's problematic cards is that they're white:

- Swords to Plowshares;
- Seal of Cleansing;
- Parallax Wave;
- Opalescence;
- Meddling Mage;
- Abeyance;
- Replenish itself.

Gloom makes it much harder for the opponent to chain their interactions and develop the combo plan. If you can resolve it, it can dramatically slow down the Replenish deck and open a very favorable window for you.

It isn't a card that always wins by itself, but it's one of the few sideboard cards that attacks many of the opponent's most important axes at once.

## Enchantment Removal

Enchantment removal is important because it lets you interact with their plan even after something has resolved.

If the opponent resolves **Opalescence** but hasn't yet brought back or resolved **Decree of Silence**, you can destroy Opalescence at instant speed and try to stall the board.

This can be very important: removing Opalescence can turn a lethal board into a set of enchantments that are no longer creatures, buying time or even flipping a combat step.

Enchantment removal also helps against:

- Seal of Cleansing;
- Parallax Wave;
- Solitary Confinement, if present;
- Attunement in certain spots;
- any hate permanents post-board.

You can't always answer everything, but having access to Naturalize, Ray, or Monk Realist lets you not depend exclusively on speed.

## Tormod's Crypt

Some opponents can have **Tormod's Crypt** post-board.

It isn't always the most problematic card, but it has to be respected because it can interfere with your Hermit lines and with some Survival lines. Naturalize and Uktabi Orangutan are useful precisely because they can also answer this kind of card.

If your plan goes through the graveyard and you have no way to remove Crypt, it can be correct to name it with Cabal Therapy or build an alternative plan.

## Attunement and Frantic Search

One of the reasons the matchup is hard to read is that the opponent's hand can change very quickly.

Cards like **Attunement** and **Frantic Search** let Replenish see many cards, discard combo pieces, and turn an apparently slow hand into a lethal hand.

This means the information you get from Duress or Cabal Therapy is important, but doesn't always stay valid for many turns. You can see a hand without Replenish, and then the opponent can dig quickly and find it.

For this reason, when you open a window, you have to try to capitalize on it. You can't always afford to wait many turns after stripping a key card.

## Ancient Tomb and Counting Mana

Another important detail is the presence of **Ancient Tomb** and a high land count.

Replenish often runs 25 or 26 lands, including sol lands like Ancient Tomb. This means it can generate extra mana suddenly and pull off sequences that looked impossible a turn earlier.

When you count the opponent's clock, you always have to consider the possibility that Ancient Tomb accelerates:

- Attunement;
- Parallax Wave;
- Opalescence;
- Replenish;
- a double spell;
- Meddling Mage plus protection.

You shouldn't reason only in terms of untapped lands, but also in terms of the quality of mana those lands can produce.

## Avoiding the Topdeck War

In a topdeck war, Replenish is probably stronger than you.

The reason is that many of its single draws can be extremely powerful. A Replenish at the right moment can recover an almost-lost game; Attunement or Frantic Search can rebuild the hand; a single white card can interact with your plan.

For this reason, when possible, you don't want to bring the game to a pure topdeck war. You want to use discard and combo pressure to force a window and close before the opponent has too many draw steps.

It's of course easier said than done, because Replenish has many redundant cards and can recover quickly. But as a general principle, if you have the chance to close with a reasonably protected line, it's often better to do so than to stall too long.

## How to Approach the Matchup

The matchup has to be played balancing two needs:

- applying pressure with your combo pieces;
- making sure those pieces stay around long enough to win.

It isn't enough to be fast if you lose to a single Swords, Seal, or Abeyance. But you also can't wait too long, because Replenish can assemble a kill very quickly and is very dangerous in a topdeck war.

Discard is the glue between these two plans. It lets you figure out whether you can force Hermit, whether Survival is safe, whether you have to respect Seal, whether the opponent has Abeyance, or whether you have to prepare for Meddling Mage.

Often the approach depends on what you see with Duress or Therapy. But you have to remember that Attunement and Frantic Search can change the state of the game quickly, so information has to be used fast.

## Matchup Summary

Replenish is a matchup very close to fifty-fifty.

You're both fast combo decks, but Replenish has the advantage that many of its combo cards naturally interact with your plans.

The keys to the matchup are:

- be fast, but not blind;
- use Cabal Therapy based on the plan: often Swords, but Seal if the plan is Survival;
- respect Abeyance in Hermit lines;
- don't underestimate Meddling Mage post-board;
- bring in additional discard;
- bring in enchantment removal;
- consider Gloom one of the best cards in the matchup;
- remember that Replenish can change its hand very quickly with Attunement and Frantic Search;
- don't drag the game too far toward a topdeck war;
- always count the extra mana from Ancient Tomb.

In short: against Replenish it isn't enough to win the race. You have to win the race through cards that are, at the same time, combo pieces and interactions against you.

It's a very technical matchup because every card of the opponent's can have multiple roles. Seal isn't just protection. Opalescence isn't just a kill. Parallax Wave isn't just a combo piece. And Abeyance isn't just a tempo effect: against Hermit Druid, it can be a real kill.
